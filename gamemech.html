Brief

This project focuses on developing a clear understanding of the term "Game Mechanics" in academic and industry contexts, using clustering algorithms to analyze qualitative data and uncover patterns efficiently.

Roles: Qualitative User Researcher and Data Analyst
Year: 2022-2023
Team: 2 Researcher and 2 Faculty Mentor
Tools: Figma, Excel Sheet, Visual Studio Code
Programming Language: Python

Problem Statement

The term "game mechanics" is widely used across game design, development, and player communities but remains ambiguous and lacks a universally accepted definition. Game mechanics are generally understood to refer to the rules, systems, or interactions that drive gameplay, yet their scope and meaning vary significantly depending on the context in which they are discussed. For example, game mechanics can be interpreted as anything from specific actions a player can take (e.g., jumping, shooting) to broader frameworks like resource management or narrative branching. This lack of a clear and consistent definition leads to confusion in both academic and industry discussions, making it difficult to accurately communicate and evaluate game design processes.

Solution

This project adopts a mixed-method research approach, to develop an understanding of the term "Game Mechanics" by exploring its interpretations in academic and industrial contexts. Building on previous research that identified 49 varied definitions of the term “Game Mechanics” in the academic sphere, this project seeks to expand that work by analyzing these 49 and additional 22 industry-specific definitions. By using a mixed-method approach, we aim to highlight the distinct yet related interpretations between these two sectors, offering clearer insights into how "Game Mechanics" is understood and applied. The project is focused on appreciating these differences rather than arriving at a universally accepted definition.

Challenges

One of the key challenges faced during this project was the complexity and ambiguity surrounding the term "Game Mechanics." With thousands of definitions available, it was difficult to distill a manageable sample while ensuring it represented both academic and industrial viewpoints. Additionally, while thematic coding helped categorize the definitions, it was insufficient for quantifying the relationships between them. Another challenge involved the subjectivity of qualitative data, where nuances and context must be carefully analyzed, making it difficult to find patterns without relying on manual and time-intensive methods like thematic analysis.

  2. Data Analysis
    3.

    In 2021, Priscilla, David, and Carstensdottir (Lo et al., 2021) conducted a thorough exploration of the term “game mechanics” and discovered approximately 3976 definitions within the academic literature. Their analysis identified 49 explicit definitions and highlighted the ambiguity and lack of agreement among scholars. To construct a similar comprehensive sample of industry professionals, we distilled 22 definitions from a pool of approximately 4932 from three notable venues. The final dataset has 71 definitions that represent academic and industrial spheres.

    a. Qualitative Thematic Coding
    Initially, we performed thematic coding on the qualitative data( 71 definitions ) that was collected through extensive literature reviews. We used open coding to label each definition and then grouped these codes into broader thematic categories through axial coding. We observed 48 codes, which were then grouped into eight themes.

    b. K-means Clustering on Thematic Data
    To further analyze and identify patterns within the data, we applied the K-means clustering algorithm. Since K-means is designed for numerical data, we first numerically encoded the qualitative themes based on the frequency of occurrence, presence of specific codes, or other relevant metrics.

    Encoding Process: Each unit of analysis was quantified based on the codes we identified during thematic coding. This numerical representation of code allowed us to structure the data in a format suitable for K-means clustering.

    Clustering: The K-means algorithm was then applied to cluster the themes into distinct groups. Since we using this for qualitative data, we applied a modified K-means clustering algorithm that uses Levenshtein distance instead of traditional Euclidean distance. This approach allowed us to measure the linguistic similarity between themes or textual elements, clustering them based on how closely related they were in terms of textual structure rather than numerical representation. Clustering Evaluation and Selection of Optimal k: We performed an evaluation to identify the optimal number of clusters (k) by running the algorithm for k = 4 to k = 11. The goal was to identify the most accurate and meaningful clustering of the themes within our dataset. To do this, we used two key evaluation methods: t-SNE visualization and the silhouette score. t-SNE (t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding) was used to visualize the clustering results for different values of k. t-SNE is a dimensionality reduction technique that allowed us to project the high-dimensional data (in this case, the clusters of themes based on Levenshtein distance) into a 2D or 3D space. By visualizing the clusters using t-SNE, we could visually assess the separation between clusters and determine whether the clusters were well-defined or overlapped.The visualizations helped us identify whether increasing or decreasing the number of clusters led to clearer and more distinct groupings of the themes. To complement the visual assessment, we used the silhouette score to quantitatively evaluate the quality of clustering for each value of k. For each value of k, we calculated the average silhouette score across all data points to determine how well-separated the clusters were. Based on these evaluations, we determined that k = 6 provided the best balance between cluster separation and interpretability of the themes.

    c. Interpreting the Clusters
    The clusters produced by K-means helped us identify underlying patterns and relationships between themes that may not have been immediately apparent through manual analysis. Each cluster represented a group of related themes, which provided deeper insights into the main trends and commonalities in the participants' responses. From our analysis, we concluded that the term “Game Mechanics” can be understood in the context of
    1. Influencing/Changing the game
    2. Rules and Data
    3. Designing the Game
    4. Interaction and loops
    5. Player Behaviour
